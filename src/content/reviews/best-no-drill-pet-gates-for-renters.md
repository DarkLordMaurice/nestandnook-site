“No drill” is not a synonym for “no measuring,” “no marks,” or “works for every dog.” A renter-friendly gate must fit the opening, resist the actual pet, leave a usable human path, and avoid high-consequence locations where pressure mounting is inappropriate. The best gate is the lightest intervention that still creates a reliable boundary.

<h2 id="measure-first-check">Measure the opening at three heights</h2>

Measure wall-to-wall or trim-to-trim at the floor, middle, and gate height. Record baseboard depth, door swing, hinges, latch clearance, and the walking width left after installation. Then compare gate height, bar spacing, and any small pet door with every animal in the household.

## Pressure-mounted is for the right location

Pressure gates wedge between firm opposing surfaces and avoid basic drilling. Current tested-gate guidance places them in doorways or at the bottom of stairs, while hardware-mounted gates are recommended for the top of stairs. Do not use renter convenience to downgrade a safety-critical location.

## Height does not solve climbing or squeezing

A taller gate may deter jumping, but bar spacing, horizontal footholds, a small pet door, and furniture beside the gate can create other routes. Watch the pet's actual behavior and keep climbable objects away.

## The human route must remain usable

Check latch operation, walk-through width, threshold rail, and whether the gate can stay open appropriately when not needed. A narrow opening or raised rail can create a daily trip problem in a small apartment.

## Wall protection requires inspection

Clean contact pads, install cups or protectors exactly as directed, and avoid uncontrolled overtightening. Adhesive protectors can also lift paint. No product should be described as guaranteed damage-free.

## Freestanding gates are movable, not equally secure

A wood freestanding gate can divide an open room without wall pressure, but it is easier to push, climb, or bypass. Use it only where failure would be inconvenient rather than dangerous.

<h2 id="do-not-buy-if">Do not buy a no-drill pet gate if…</h2>

- The location is at the top of stairs or another high-consequence hazard.
- The opening falls outside the exact supported width and extension range.
- The pet can climb, jump, push, or squeeze through the format.
- Baseboards or angled walls prevent even pressure contact.
- The walk-through opening or threshold creates an unacceptable trip risk.

Use the gate as one layer inside [the rental pet-proofing checklist](/pet-care/how-to-pet-proof-a-rental-apartment/), not as a substitute for removing hazards.

## Frequently asked questions

**Are pressure-mounted pet gates safe at the top of stairs?**  
No-drill pressure-mounted gates are generally intended for doorways or the bottom of stairs, not the top. Current gate guidance recommends hardware-mounted barriers at the top of stairs. Follow the exact product instructions.

**How do I measure for a pressure-mounted gate?**  
Measure the opening at the top, middle, and bottom, account for baseboards and trim, and use only the manufacturer's supported width and extension combinations. The mounting pads need firm opposing surfaces.

**Will a no-drill gate damage apartment walls?**  
Pressure pads and wall cups may still mark paint or trim, especially when overtightened, dirty, or used with adhesive protectors. Follow installation instructions, inspect contact points, and do not promise damage-free results.

**Do freestanding gates work for every dog?**  
No. They are less secure than pressure- or hardware-mounted gates and suit smaller or calmer pets better. A strong, tall, climbing, or determined pet may move or bypass them.

> **Winnie’s take:** A pressure gate is renter-friendly. A pressure gate balanced on two baseboards and optimism is performance art. Measure the opening where the pads actually touch.
