I was supposed to bring a side dish and a folding chair. That was the entire scope of my responsibility at Saturday's backyard barbecue, agreed upon in advance, written nowhere but understood by everyone. Then the friend actually running the smoker got pulled into a work call that would not end, looked at me with the specific desperation of someone who has 20 pounds of pork shoulder on the line, and handed me the tongs. Hosting a backyard bbq, it turns out, has an emergency-substitute clause nobody tells you about until you're the substitute.

## I know nothing about smokers and it did not matter

Here's the thing about being handed someone else's smoker mid-cook: you are not starting from zero, you're starting from hour three of someone else's careful plan, and your entire job is to not ruin it. I did not know the internal temperature target. I did not know how long the wood chunks had been in. What I did know — because apparently this transfers from every other domain of my life — was how to ask good questions fast, write down what I was told, and not touch anything I wasn't sure about until I understood it.

## Letting go of "my job today" was the actual lesson

I'd shown up with a very specific, very small plan for my own Saturday, and the moment that plan changed, my instinct was to feel mildly panicked about it — not because tending a smoker is hard, exactly, but because it wasn't what I'd organized myself to expect. The barbecue didn't care about my plan. It needed someone to watch the temperature and keep the door closed, and I was the person standing there with functioning hands.

## Good smoke, good people, genuinely good times

By the time the pork shoulder came off — hours later, considerably more tender than I take credit for — nobody at that table cared who technically ran the smoker. They cared that dinner was excellent and the company was better. I went home smelling like a campfire and thinking about how much of my own stress, on an average day, comes from clinging to a plan a situation has already quietly moved past. The smoker didn't teach me barbecue. It taught me the plan was never actually the point.
