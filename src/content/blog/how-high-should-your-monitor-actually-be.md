"How high should your monitor actually be" is one of those questions that wants a number back — six inches, four inches, exactly at eye level — and every time I dig into what buyers actually report, the honest answer is "it depends on furniture you already own," which is a deeply unsatisfying thing to tell someone holding a tape measure. I spent a decade reading spec sheets for a regional home-goods chain before this job, and the number-chasing instinct is exactly the thing I used to watch shoppers walk into over and over: they wanted the one true measurement, when what they actually needed was the right test for their own setup.

## Why there's no universal number

Your correct monitor height is the sum of four things that are different for every desk in the country: your chair height, your desk height, your own torso and neck length, and whatever height your monitor happens to sit at out of the box. Change any one of those — a new chair, a desk you inherited instead of bought, a monitor that came with a shorter stand than your last one — and the "right" number moves with it. That's why a flat recommendation like "raise your screen four inches" is frequently wrong for the specific person reading it, even though it might be exactly right for whoever the advice was originally written for. [The actual reliable method](/home-office/monitor-height-ergonomics-guide/) is a ten-second eye-level test you run at your own desk, not a number you copy from somewhere else — worth doing before you buy anything, since it tells you how much lift you actually need instead of guessing.

## The dual-monitor version nobody warns you about

Here's the complaint that shows up specifically in dual-monitor reviews and nowhere else: one screen gets raised, the other doesn't, and now the neck isn't tipping forward evenly — it's dipping sideways, repeatedly, toward whichever monitor sits lower. That's a meaningfully different strain pattern than the single-monitor version, and in my read of enough of these reviews, it's an easy trap to fall into precisely because most general monitor-height advice assumes one screen. People buy a riser for their main display, fix that one, and don't think to check whether their second monitor — often an older one, often on a different stand, sometimes just propped on whatever was lying around — is sitting at a matching height. The fix isn't a taller riser. It's a matched pair, which is a completely different shopping problem than most people realize they're solving.

## What reviewers actually say moves the needle

Reading through enough buyer feedback on stands and risers, adjustability shows up far more often as the actual satisfaction driver than raw maximum height does. A stand that only goes up to one fixed level is a bet that your setup never changes; a stand or arm with real height range is a hedge against the next monitor, chair, or desk you own. For two mismatched monitors specifically, independent-adjustment mounts get singled out constantly for exactly the reason above — each screen can land at its own correct height instead of both being forced onto one shared platform. [The monitor stand roundup](/home-office/best-monitor-stands-for-dual-monitors/) breaks down which of the current picks handle that matched-height problem versus which are better suited to a single, larger display — a distinction that's easy to miss in a product photo but obvious the first week you're using the wrong one.

## When it's not actually about the monitor

If you've run the eye-level test, matched both screens, and the ache is still there by evening, that's usually a sign the actual culprit is somewhere else in the chain — chair height, seat depth, or how far back you're sitting — not a case for buying a third riser and hoping. The [Home Office & Ergonomics hub](/home-office/) is the better next stop for tracking that down, since monitor height is one variable in a setup with several. And if anything radiates or doesn't resolve with rest, that's a question for an actual doctor, not a blog about desks — what I'm describing here is the common, purely mechanical version, which for most people staring at a mismatched two-monitor setup, is exactly what it is.
